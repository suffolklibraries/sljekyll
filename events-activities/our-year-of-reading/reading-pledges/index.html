“Not to read more - already addicted and housework suffers - but to read more widely.

Reading has always been important to me; no children nearby. Children's Hour on radio, 5-6pm brought Uncle Mac and Toytown and I still have a Toytown Annual; also a Princess Elizabeth Gift Book. Well meaning relatives gave me Coral Island, Children of the New Forest, Treasure Island, Robinson Crusoe, and both volumes of Alice. No Blytons; in the thirties she had probably made no impact on older people. I spent birthday money on Mickey Mouse and Rupert.

The local library opened when I was seven, with Arthur Ransome, Brent-Dyer and the Jolly Books, annuals edited, I think, by Herbert and Eleanor Farjeon,. Evacuation, in 1939, where I read Angela Brazil, and The Pilgrim's Progress, and after School Certificate in an underground shelter with V1s overhead, to relatives where I discovered crime anthologies and the Wild West Magazine.

When I left school at 16 my father had retired, money and University places were hard to come by, I had two choices which would enable me to 'learn and earn'; Nursing and Librarianship. This has led to a career in libraries, and in retirement a developing interest in writing, shared with a variety of new friends in the Sudbury Library Top Time group, resurrected in 2015. For me, reading and writing are essential to each other.”

‒ G A Proctor

“I think reading is important because it gives us multiple contexts within which we can form our ideal selves. Through adventures we can learn to be brave, by reading mysteries we can learn how to observe and puzzle, we discover our own moralities, our notions of right and wrong, explore our emotions, and it all happens in the privacy of our own imaginations. The experience is utterly personal and profoundly enriching.

I pledge to always champion libraries, because it's where I found all the books of my childhood, and I pledge to tell as many people as possible about every book I read and enjoy.”

‒ M G Leonard
